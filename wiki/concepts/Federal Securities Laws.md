# Federal Securities Laws

The body of U.S. statutes the [[U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission|SEC]] administers. The unifying principle is **full disclosure**: investors should receive accurate, material information and be protected from fraud. (Source: [[investor.gov - Laws That Govern the Securities Industry]])

---

## The core statutes

- **Securities Act of 1933** - the "truth in securities" law. Requires registration and a prospectus for public offerings and prohibits fraud and misrepresentation in securities sales. Disclosure here is largely a **one-time** event tied to an offering. (Source: [[investor.gov - Laws That Govern the Securities Industry]])
- **Securities Exchange Act of 1934** - created the SEC; governs **secondary markets**. Requires ongoing periodic reporting (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K), regulates exchanges/brokers/dealers, and mandates beneficial-ownership disclosure above 5%. (Source: [[Wikipedia - Securities Exchange Act of 1934]])
- **Trust Indenture Act of 1939** - sets standards for debt securities (bonds) and issuer–bondholder indentures.
- **Investment Company Act of 1940** - regulates mutual funds and similar vehicles; mandates disclosure of financial condition and investment policies.
- **Investment Advisers Act of 1940** - requires paid securities advisers to register with the SEC and follow investor-protection rules.
- **Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002** - post-Enron reforms; strengthened corporate financial responsibility and created the PCAOB.
- **Dodd-Frank Act of 2010** - broad post-2008 financial-regulation overhaul (consumer protection, trading limits, governance, transparency).
- **JOBS Act of 2012** - eased capital-raising rules for emerging-growth companies.

## The 1933 vs 1934 distinction

The two foundational acts split by market stage:

- **1933 Act → primary market** (the offering itself): register before you sell.
- **1934 Act → secondary market** (ongoing trading): keep disclosing while public.

This is why an IPO files an [[SEC Filing Types|S-1]] (1933 Act) but then files 10-Ks and 10-Qs forever after (1934 Act). (Source: [[investor.gov - Laws That Govern the Securities Industry]])
