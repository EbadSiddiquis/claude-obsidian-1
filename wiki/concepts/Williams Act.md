# Williams Act

The **Williams Act of 1968** amended the [[Federal Securities Laws|Securities Exchange Act of 1934]] to regulate **tender offers and large stock accumulations**, ending the era of surprise "takeover raids." Its philosophy: give shareholders **adequate information, adequate time, equal treatment, and withdrawal rights**. (Source: [[US Law Explained - The Williams Act]])

---

## The statutory sections

- **Section 13(d)** - anyone acquiring **more than 5%** of a registered class must disclose ownership and intent on a **Schedule 13D** (or short-form 13G). (Source: [[US Law Explained - The Williams Act]])
- **Section 14(d)** - procedural rules for a **[[Tender Offer]]** for more than 5% of a registered class (implemented by **Regulation 14D**).
- **Section 14(e)** - a broad **anti-fraud** rule for "any tender offer" (implemented by **Regulation 14E**); it reaches offers even outside 14(d)'s registration trigger.

## Why it matters

Before 1968 a raider could pressure shareholders to sell fast with little information. The Williams Act made takeovers a disclosed, time-boxed, equal-treatment process - the foundation of modern M&A practice and the [[Tender Offer]] rules.

## Confidence

**high** - the section structure and 5% threshold are codified and corroborated by SEC primary guidance.
