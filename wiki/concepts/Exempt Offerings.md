# Exempt Offerings

The [[Federal Securities Laws|Securities Act of 1933]] requires registration to sell securities **unless an exemption applies**. The exempt-offering framework is the menu of ways companies raise capital without a full SEC registration. (Source: [[SEC.gov - Private Placements Rule 506]])

---

## The menu

| Exemption | 12-month cap | Who can invest | Solicitation | Key filing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Regulation D]] Rule 506(b) | unlimited | accredited + 35 sophisticated | no | Form D |
| [[Regulation D]] Rule 506(c) | unlimited | accredited only (verified) | yes | Form D |
| [[Regulation A]] Tier 1 | $20M | anyone | yes | Form 1-A |
| [[Regulation A]] Tier 2 | $75M | anyone (limits for non-accredited) | yes | Form 1-A |
| [[Regulation Crowdfunding]] | $5M | anyone (investor caps) | via portal | Form C |
| Section 4(a)(2) | unlimited | private, no public offering | no | none |

## How to read it

- **Reg D** = the private workhorse (no public marketing in 506(b); verified-accredited-only in 506(c)).
- **Reg A** = a "mini-IPO" open to the public, with SEC qualification and (Tier 2) state preemption.
- **Reg CF** = retail crowdfunding through a registered [[Funding Portal]].
- **[[Rule 144]]** is the exit: it governs when securities bought in these exempt deals can be **resold** to the public.

## The trade-off axis

More public reach (Reg A, Reg CF) buys broader investor access at the cost of disclosure and filing burden; more privacy (Reg D, 4(a)(2)) buys speed and light filing at the cost of a narrower, wealthier investor pool.
